Accessibility in AI: Speech Recognition & Neurodiversity

Using AI-powered speech recognition can completely transform accessibility. Tools such as voice assistants, dictation tools, and real-time transcription capable of aiding users in their day-to-day lives. However, for neurodivgent users-- like those with autism, speech processing disorders, or ADHD-- these tools can often be more frustrating than helpful, potentially causing added confusion or overstimulation.

Over the course of this semester, we learned that true accessibility in technology requires not only technical solutions, but also a shift in perspective-- moving from designing for the “average” user to embracing the full spectrum of human diversity. This means recognizing that neurodivergent users, with their unique means of communicating and processing information, must be consciously included in both the dataset training these AI models, as well as the teams building them. By centering inclusivity, we ensure that AI-driven tools genuinely empower all users, rather than reinforcing existing barriers.

Neurodivergent users often utilitze rhythms, inflections, and phrasing during speech that are often underrepresented in traditional training sets. When AI models are trained primarily on “neurotypical” speech, they may misinterpret neurodivergent users’ words and input, leading to incorrect responses and repeated failures. And if the result is technology that alienates those it could benefit most, then clearly we as engineers have failed to execute an inclusive design.

For example, an individual with echolalia-- a condition often seen in autism where phrases are repeated out of context-- may frequently be misinterpreted by AI, causing the system to get stuck in loops and be more of a burden than a boon. Users with ADHD, on the other hand, may speak quickly or change topics fast, with traditional speech models often struggling to keep up or maintain context.

Applying a "virtue ethics" lens helps here. Instead of only asking what maximizes utility (as in Bentham-Mill's utilitarianism) or what established rules to follow (like deontology), we should focus on character and compassion. An ethical technologist should strive to build systems structured around patience, empathy, and adaptability-- not perfection.

Real systems like Google’s Project Euphonia are beginning to address this gap by training models on a more diverse set of voice patterns. While this is important progress, we believe this must become standard practise, not an isolated experiment.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES: ACCESSIBILITY IN AI

Explore case studies that show how AI-powered tools and inclusive design can transform accessibility for neurodiverse users. These real-world examples highlight both the challenges and successes in making technology more inclusive.

Google: Project Euphonia

Many people with speech impairments or atypical speech patterns-- such as those with ALS, Down syndrome, or autism-- struggle to use mainstream speech recognition tools, which are typically trained on neurotypical voices.

How AI/Assistive Tech Was Used: Google’s Project Euphonia uses deep learning to train speech recognition models on thousands of voice samples from people with diverse speech patterns. By including their voices, the models become better at recognizing and transcribing atypical speech.

Impact/Outcome: Project Euphonia has made it possible for people with speech impairments to use voice assistants and dictation software more reliably, increasing their independence and digital access. It also sets a new standard for inclusive AI design by involving users with disabilities in the development process.

Microsoft: Immersive Reader

Students with dyslexia and other reading disabilities often struggle to access and comprehend written material, limiting their participation in mainstream classrooms.

How AI/Assistive Tech Was Used: Microsoft’s Immersive Reader uses AI to read text aloud, break words into syllables, and adjust text spacing and background color on challenging, text-heavy pages. These features help users decode and process content with greater ease.

Impact/Outcome: Immersive Reader has improved reading comprehension and engagement for students with dyslexia and other learning/processing differences. It demonstrates how AI can be tailored to support neurodiverse learners and foster greater inclusion in education.

Practical Takeaways


Recommendation Why It Matters
AI speech tools should be trained on a wider range of voices, including neurodivergent users. Ensures speech recognition works for everyone, not just neurotypical users.
Developmental teams must consult with neurodivergent individuals during design. Direct feedback leads to more inclusive and effective solutions.
Encourage participatory design by involving neurodivergent users in user testing and feedback cycles. Identifies real-world challenges and usability issues early.
Accessibility isn’t just about adding features; it’s about rethinking the default user model. Prevents exclusion by addressing systemic design biases.
Regularly audit AI systems for unintended bias, and update models as new patterns of use emerge. Keeps technology fair and adaptive to changing needs.
Provide customizable user settings that allow individuals to tailor AI interactions to their sensory and communication preferences. Empowers users to adjust tools for their unique needs.
Question to ask: “Who is being left out by this design, and how do we center them instead?” Keeps the focus on inclusion throughout the development process.
