AI-PUBLIC-INTEREST

What are some of the applications of AI to public-interest?

Potential Applications & their implications

Artificial Intelligence (AI) has the power to transform society enhancing healthcare, education, public safety, and access to resources. But who benefits from these technologies? And who bears the risks? When AI systems are developed and deployed primarily for profit or without inclusive design, they can reinforce systemic inequality, threaten privacy, and undermine public trust. As responsible technologists, we must ensure AI serves the broader public interest not just private stakeholders. Using philosopher John Rawls’ concept of the “original position” and the “veil of ignorance,” we can evaluate AI systems by asking: Would I agree to this system if I didn’t know whether I would benefit or be harmed by it? Rawls’ Difference Principle also suggests that inequalities in outcomes (e.g., who benefits from an AI system) are only justified if they improve the position of the least advantaged. Applying this, AI should be developed in a way that prioritizes fairness, accessibility, and accountability. Predictive policing tools use historical crime data to identify potential hotspots or suspects. However, these tools often reflect biased data, leading to over-policing in communities of color. From a Rawlsian perspective, such tools fail the fairness test: if you didn’t know your race or neighborhood, you likely wouldn’t agree to the deployment of a biased system. Reforming or rejecting such tools is necessary when they do not uplift or protect the least advantaged.
